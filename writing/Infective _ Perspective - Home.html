Of Macroscopic and Microscopic: Haiti, 2010-Present


In 2010, a devastating earthquake struck Haiti, prompting massive relief efforts from around the world. Unfortunately, however, help for a large-scale natural disaster brought harm in the form of an equally dangerous, yet microscopic killer: a cholera outbreak soon hit, killing over 770,000 Haitians, nearly 8 percent of the country’s population. This outbreak, still causing flare-ups today, is the largest cholera epidemic in modern history. 

​The origin of the Haitian cholera outbreak was heavily disputed for months. One camp argued that the earthquake perturbed Haiti’s natural environment and could thus easily have brought a lurking germ out of hiding. After all, environmental disruptions have frequently been attributed to infectious disease outbreaks all over the world. Meanwhile, other parties argued that humans introduced cholera to Haiti when they arrived in the country for relief work.

Why do we care about vaccinating children against measles if measles isn't a severe illness?

Contact Us to submit a question!

​It has long been known that the measles virus suppresses one’s immune system during and immediately following infection. Recently, however, studies have determined that this state of so-called "immune amnesia" may actually last for years.
 
During its disease course, the measles virus destroys its host's B cells and T cells— the main adaptive and memory components of your immune system. Because of this, patients become particularly susceptible to opportunistic infections; that is, pathogens that otherwise typically pose no threat to healthy individuals.
 
While the measles virus itself may abate within one or two weeks, it takes much longer for one’s immune defenses to be restored to normalcy. As a result, infection susceptibility remains elevated for that time.
 
In support of animal studies that revealed several years of immune deficiency in measles-challenged monkeys, a retrospective study on humans further demonstrated a correlation between deaths from infections and number of measles cases. Simply put, in years marked by fewer cases of the measles, fewer children died from other opportunistic or non-opportunistic infections. Conversely, years that exhibited a high caseload of measles were marked by an increase in child death due to infections.
 
A vaccine against measles goes beyond its namesake: it is a vaccine against a myriad of infections. By preventing measles infections altogether, we can avoid immunosuppression and thus decrease child mortality as a whole. Taking into account these effects, the measles vaccine offers an elegant solution to a source of morbidity and mortality worldwide. 

-Alessandra Tomasi is a first-year medical student at the University of North Carolina in Chapel Hill

I had a friend who asked if she could get the flu shot twice in one season. Is that effective or detrimental? 

Contact Us to submit a question!

When it comes to double-dosing on vaccines, some people worry that they are overloading their immune system and that they will get sick as a result. On the other side of the camp, some think that being immunized twice will boost their immune response and protect them even more from the flu.

The answer to the question, however, is neither. According to the CDC: "[i]n adults, studies have not shown a benefit from getting more than one dose of vaccine during the same influenza season, even among elderly persons with weakened immune systems. Except for some children, only one dose of flu vaccine is recommended each season."

Interestingly, when it comes to immunocompromised people such as the very young children or elderly adults mentioned above, the CDC does in fact recommend increased doses. For children between 6 months and 8 years old, they recommend two doses. The first one serves to "prime" the immune system, and the second dose - administered at least 28 days after the first one - provides the actual immune protection that season. For older adults, instead, Fluzone is a high (single) dose version of the seasonal flu vaccine. It is designed for individuals 65 and older, and contains four times the amount of antigen (immune-inducing material) compared to the regular flu shot.

Artemisia annua. Image from Wikipedia

Artemisia annua is a relatively ubiquitous species of sweet wormwood whose use dates back to Chinese traditional medicine. Blurring the Line Between Communicable and Non-Communicable Diseases

In 2010, the World Health Organization (WHO) published a report highlighting various aspects of non-communicable diseases (NCD) in low- and middle-income countries. The piece points to four main causes of death in this category of illnesses: cancer, cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and chronic respiratory disease. Four major risk factors are then enumerated: tobacco use, physical inactivity, unhealthy diet and harmful use of alcohol. With regards to cancer, the report notes that the types of cancer prevalent worldwide vary according to the underlying risks associated with each respective country. Though the four aforementioned major risk factors still apply, there are other contributors to the rise of cancer, such as infectious agents: namely, viruses. While the WHO’s report goes on to mention how infectious agents account for nearly eighteen percent of all cancers worldwide, it fails to explain how that number disproportionately underestimates the cancers found in Africa, many more of which are virus-related.

Dawn of the Planet of the Superbugs

With names like “superbug”, “nightmare bacteria”, and “the phantom menace”, carbapenem-resistant Enterobacteriacaeae, abbreviated as CRE, seem to be lifted from the pages of a comic book. Making recent headlines with two confirmed cases of this superbug in the United States this past year, what exactly are CRE and what is the cause for concern? Enterobacteriaceae are a family of bacteria most frequently found in our intestines. Zoonotic Diseases and the Domestication of Animals

Infections that pass from animals to people are known as zoonoses. The causative agents for these diseases can be bacteria, parasites, viruses, fungi or even more unconventional agents (for instance, Mad Cow Disease is called by a prion, which is essentially a disease-causing protein). Despite the rise of novel infections in the 21st century, zoonotic diseases as a whole are nothing new. They date back to the beginning of human history when ancient humans came into contact with animals. One of the earliest historical records of a zoonotic outbreak dates back to 430-425 B.C.E., when typhus took over Athens and likely played a pivotal role in the outcome of the Peloponnesian War. After the Plague of Athens, other zoonoses made their way around the globe for millennia: Yellow Fever, malaria and rabies are just some examples.

How to Edit Genes (Using CRISPR)

Image from ScienceNews.org
Last month, you read an overview on CRISPR and its potential in the scientific and medical world. But how does CRISPR work on a molecular level? The CRISPR system allows selective permanent editing of almost any genomic region, whether by deletion, insertion or substitution of specific nucleotides (units of DNA). CRISPR utilizes a short RNA molecule (similar to DNA, but used by cells to put genes in their active form) that is complementary to the genomic region of interest, which targets the DNA for cleavage by the Cas9 protein. CRISPR/Cas9 therefore has almost unlimited potential, not just for laboratory research studies but more importantly for potentially curing genetic diseases.

Your immune system is what protects your body from attack by the multitude of bacteria, viruses, and parasites that seek to make your body their home. A Brief Overview of the Immune System

Antibody. Image from Cambridge University
The immune system comprises a uniquely vast network of organs, tissues, and cells that work together to fight off foreign bodies in a host. Any foreign body—a bacterium, virus, parasite, or even a damaged host cell— that in this way elicits an immune response is known as an ‘antigen.’ Specifically, an invader such as a microbe or tumor cell will contain unique structural features that are recognized by the host immune system as foreign; that is, as something that does not belong. ​An understanding of the many intricacies of the immune system therefore begins with the discrimination between “self” and “non-self.” Simply put, the body's immune system will ignore any cell that carries a "self" marker molecule, but will instead be triggered as soon as a cell or organism presents with a "non-self" (or, foreign) marker.

Bacteria: An Unlikely Source for Cancer Therapy

Cancer is a disease that affects millions of people across the world. It is caused by abnormal cells in the body that divide uncontrollably; metastasis more specifically describes this uncontrolled cell growth spreading to other parts of the body. Over the years, many novel cancer therapies have been studied and developed including surgical resection, radiotherapy, and chemotherapy. These treatments have been found to be effective for many cancer patients around the world and saved thousands of lives every year. Surgery, radiotherapy, and chemotherapy all carry their own inherent risks and potentially damaging side effects; that’s why a less invasive yet equally powerful treatment against a variety of cancers is a holy grail in oncology. Therefore, researchers are always trying to find other forms of anti-cancer therapy. You’ll see that some seem to come a bit more out of left field than others.

Three years ago, a 2-year-old who had recently returned from a trip to India arrived at the Johns Hopkins Children’s Center with persistent fever and malaise. A chest x-ray showed a lung abnormality, and after several difficult tests she was diagnosed with extensively drug-resistant tuberculosis (XDR-TB). Diagnosing her illness was difficult, because TB tests typically require large amounts of sputum that children cannot produce. Treatment was going to be even more difficult, given her age and the multi-drug resistance of her infection. Enter Sanjay Jain, JHU pediatrician and tuberculosis expert. His team of researchers has been working on innovative, real-time imaging techniques to track infectious diseases in patients and monitor bacterial behavior.

The World Health Organization recently published a press release outlining a colossal public health achievement: meningitis A has been nearly eliminated in Africa through vaccination, which has managed to reach over 235 million people. Five years after it was first used in the "meningitis belt," cases of the deadly infectious disease have gone from over 250,000 in 1996 to a mere 80 cases in 2015. These 80 cases occurred in countries that have not yet performed mass immunization campaigns among unvaccinated populations.
Spreading of viruses like Hepatitis C and HIV is not restricted solely to those physically sharing a contaminated needle— it also extends to their partners and children through sexual and perinatal transmissions. In an attempt to combat these statistics, needle exchange programs have been implemented in several cities around the United States.
Hepatitis C (HCV) is a virus that primarily affects the liver. Though it is generally asymptomatic, chronic infection can lead to liver failure, liver cancer, or other life-threatening conditions that typically require a liver transplant. For a long time, standard treatment for HCV required 48 weeks of weekly interferon injections coupled with oral antiviral pills. Unsurprisingly from such long-term treatments, side effects were the norm, and many people either stopped or refused treatment. Everything changed in 2013 – or at least, it should have.
